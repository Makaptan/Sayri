Bird of Prey

Bird of Prey, the Cornell Lab of Ornithology's inaugural feature-length documentary, offers a deeply intimate and compelling exploration into the life, behavior, and urgent conservation of the Great Philippine Eagle—one of the rarest, most powerful, and most majestic raptors on the planet. Directed by acclaimed filmmaker Eric Liner and featuring the work of renowned wildlife cinematographer Neil Rettig, the film beautifully captures the awe-inspiring presence of this extraordinary bird amidst the rapidly vanishing old-growth forests of the Philippines, a critical habitat that is under constant threat from deforestation and human encroachment. The documentary takes viewers on a gripping journey that chronicles Neil Rettig’s return to the Philippines after several decades, reigniting his personal and professional passion for the Philippine Eagle. Rettig embarks on a challenging and often perilous expedition deep into remote mountain forests, using his expert skills to document the eagle’s elusive behavior in its natural environment—a feat rarely achieved due to the bird’s reclusive nature and dwindling numbers. The film intimately portrays Rettig’s perseverance, the physical and emotional toll of his mission, and the rare moments of connection between human and raptor that underline the profound significance of this species. Alongside Rettig, the documentary highlights the unwavering dedication of a passionate team from the Philippine Eagle Foundation—scientists, conservationists, and local community members—who work tirelessly to protect the eagle from extinction. Through innovative captive breeding programs, critical habitat restoration efforts, and extensive public education campaigns, these individuals embody hope and resilience in the face of environmental adversity. Their stories are interwoven with powerful narratives of community involvement, illustrating how indigenous and rural populations living near the eagle’s habitats are becoming vital partners in conservation, balancing cultural traditions with sustainable practices. Beyond showcasing breathtaking cinematography that reveals the eagle’s majestic flight and intricate behaviors, Bird of Prey also functions as a call to action, emphasizing the urgent need for collective global responsibility in preserving biodiversity. With fewer than 400 breeding pairs remaining in the wild, the Great Philippine Eagle is classified as critically endangered, making it a symbol of broader ecological fragility in the region. The film underscores how habitat destruction, illegal logging, and human activities have accelerated the species’ decline, and it challenges viewers to reflect on the impact of humanity’s actions on vulnerable ecosystems. Critically acclaimed on the international film circuit, Bird of Prey has received multiple awards and commendations for its storytelling, cinematography, and conservation message. It has sparked meaningful conversations among environmentalists, policymakers, educators, and the general public, helping to elevate awareness about wildlife conservation and the specific plight of the Philippine Eagle. The documentary’s accessibility through major streaming platforms like Amazon Prime Video and YouTube has allowed it to reach a wide and diverse audience worldwide, inspiring viewers to support conservation initiatives, participate in advocacy, and recognize the power of individual and community action in protecting endangered species. In sum, Bird of Prey is more than a nature documentary—it is a poignant testament to hope, resilience, and the enduring bond between humans and the natural world. It invites audiences to witness the beauty and vulnerability of one of the world’s most extraordinary raptors and to join the urgent movement to secure its survival for future generations.
